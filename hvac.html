Section 1

HVAC System Types

Understanding which system you have — or which is best for your situation — is the foundation of proper HVAC care.

Central Split System
The most common residential setup in DFW. Separate indoor air handler and outdoor condenser connected by refrigerant lines. Handles both cooling and heating (with gas furnace or heat strips).
Most Common $3,500–$8,000 installed 15–20 yr lifespan
Packaged Unit
All components (compressor, condenser, evaporator) in one single outdoor cabinet. Common in older DFW homes and light commercial. Sits on a rooftop or concrete slab.
All-in-One $4,000–$9,000 installed 12–17 yr lifespan
Mini-Split (Ductless)
No ductwork required. One outdoor unit connected to up to 8 indoor wall-mounted heads. Each zone is independently controlled. Ideal for additions, garages, and older homes without ducts.
Highest SEER $2,500–$7,000/zone 20+ yr lifespan
Heat Pump
Moves heat rather than generating it — extracts heat from outside air (even cold air) for heating and reverses in summer for cooling. Very efficient in DFW's mild winters. Contains a reversing valve.
Very Efficient $4,500–$10,000 installed 15–20 yr lifespan
Geothermal Heat Pump
Uses stable underground temperatures (55–65°F) for extreme efficiency. Ground loops circulate water/refrigerant. COP of 3–5 (300–500% efficiency). Very high upfront cost but lowest operating cost.
Highest Efficiency $15,000–$35,000 25+ yr lifespan
VRF/VRV System
Variable Refrigerant Flow/Volume — commercial-grade multi-zone system. One large outdoor unit serves many indoor units simultaneously in cooling AND heating mode. Used in hotels, offices, multi-story buildings.
Commercial $20,000–$100,000+ 20+ yr lifespan
RTU — Rooftop Unit
Self-contained packaged unit mounted on commercial rooftops. Feeds conditioned air through ductwork to the space below. Available in 2–150+ ton capacities. Standard for retail, restaurants, offices.
Commercial Standard $5,000–$40,000+ 15–20 yr lifespan
Chiller System
Large-scale commercial/industrial system that chills water circulated through the building. Uses centrifugal, screw, or scroll compressors. Paired with cooling towers and AHUs. For large buildings 50,000+ sq ft.
Large Commercial $50,000–$500,000+ 25+ yr lifespan

EPA Section 608 — Refrigerant Handling Is Regulated by Law
Only EPA-certified technicians (Section 608 certification) can purchase and handle refrigerants. It is illegal for unlicensed individuals to vent refrigerants into the atmosphere. Fines up to $44,539 per day per violation. Always use a licensed HVAC contractor.

Compressor Types — Deep Dive

The compressor is the most expensive component in any HVAC system. Understanding the type you have helps predict lifespan, efficiency, and failure modes.

1
Scroll Compressor
Most common in residential — high efficiency, quiet operation
▾
15–20yr
Avg. Lifespan
1–20 ton
Capacity Range
95%+
Volumetric Efficiency
Two spiral-shaped scrolls — one fixed, one orbiting — compress refrigerant continuously as the gas moves from the outer edge inward. No valves, no pistons. Smooth operation with minimal vibration and very low noise levels. Used in virtually all modern residential split systems and mini-splits.

Advantages: Highly efficient, quiet, fewer moving parts (longer life), handles liquid refrigerant better than reciprocating, compact design.

Common failures: Liquid slugging (liquid refrigerant enters compressor — destroys scrolls), overheating from low refrigerant, contaminants in refrigerant, worn motor windings from age. Overheating is accelerated in DFW's extreme summer conditions when outdoor temps exceed 105°F.

Volumetric Efficiency
Works like an engine: pistons move up and down in cylinders, compressing refrigerant through inlet and outlet valves. More moving parts than scroll compressors. Was the industry standard through the 1990s — still found in many older DFW homes.

Advantages: Simple design, field-repairable in some cases, handles high pressure ratios, robust construction.

Common failures: Valve failure (clicking/clanging sounds), worn piston rings (loss of compression), rod bearing failure, start capacitor failure, valve seat wear. More vibration than scroll — causes loose fittings over time.

Two intermeshing helical rotors (male and female) trap and compress refrigerant as it travels axially through the compressor. Continuous compression — no pulsating like reciprocating compressors. Found in large commercial RTUs, chillers, and industrial refrigeration.

Advantages: Excellent part-load efficiency (with slide valve modulation), long lifespan, very high capacity, handles wet refrigerant better than other types, low maintenance.

Peak Efficiency
Uses high-speed rotating impellers to impart velocity to refrigerant vapor, which is then converted to pressure in a diffuser. Dynamic compression — no contact between moving parts and housing. Used exclusively in large-tonnage water chillers for hospitals, universities, large office towers, and data centers.

Advantages: Highest efficiency at design load (best COP of any compressor type), oil-free variants available (magnetic bearings), longest lifespan, handles refrigerant well with multi-stage designs.

Common failures: Surge (unstable operation at low load), bearing wear (or magnetic bearing control failures), impeller fouling, refrigerant leaks at shaft seal (older designs), motor winding insulation breakdown. Surge is the most critical failure mode — causes severe vibration and potential catastrophic damage.

Section 6

Warning Signs & Symptoms

Every symptom tells a story. Know when to call immediately vs. when to monitor — and what to tell your technician.

Emergency — Call Now
Burning or electrical smell
Possible causes: Burning wire insulation, overheating motor windings, failing capacitor, contactor arcing, or blower motor seizing.
Shut system OFF at thermostat AND breaker. Do not restart. Call HVAC tech immediately.
Emergency — Call Now
Rotten egg / sulfur smell
Gas leak from furnace supply line or heat exchanger. Natural gas is odorized with mercaptan — rotten egg smell is the warning signal.
Leave home immediately. Don't touch any switches. Call gas company + 911 from outside.
Emergency — Call Now
No cooling in DFW summer (100°F+)
Compressor failure, capacitor failure, refrigerant leak, tripped breaker, float switch trip, thermostat failure.
Check thermostat, breaker, and drain pan. If drain pan full — clear clog. Otherwise call immediately — heat stroke risk.
Emergency — Call Now
Water actively flooding from indoor unit
Completely blocked drain line, cracked drain pan, or frozen evaporator coil melting rapidly. Can cause ceiling damage, mold, and electrical hazards.
Turn system OFF. Towel up water. Clear drain line. Call HVAC tech same day.
Urgent — Within 24–48 hrs
Loud banging or clanging
Compressor internal failure (broken connecting rod, valve), loose blower wheel hitting housing, debris inside condenser fan.
Turn off system. Operating a banging compressor causes catastrophic damage and contamination of refrigerant lines.
Urgent — Within 24–48 hrs
High-pitched squealing or screeching
Failing blower motor bearing, slipping belt (older units), condenser fan motor bearing failure, refrigerant pressure too high.
Don't ignore — bearing failure leads to motor seizure and compressor damage. Schedule service immediately.
Urgent — Within 24–48 hrs
Ice forming on indoor unit or lines
Low refrigerant charge (most common), restricted airflow (dirty filter, blocked vents, low blower speed), low outdoor temperature, malfunctioning TXV.
Turn system to FAN ONLY to thaw coil (2–4 hrs). Check filter. If ice returns after thaw, call tech — don't ignore low refrigerant.
Urgent — Within 24–48 hrs
Short cycling (turns on/off every few minutes)
Oversized system, low refrigerant, dirty coil, high-pressure lockout, thermostat in direct sunlight, refrigerant overcharge, dirty filter.
Check filter first. Short cycling causes compressor overheating and premature failure — don't operate long-term in this condition.
Urgent — Within 24–48 hrs
Sweet or chemical smell from vents
Refrigerant leak (HFCs have slight sweet ether smell). Can also be antifreeze from a leaking heat exchanger on a hydronic system.
Refrigerant leaks lose efficiency, damage compressor (runs without proper lubrication), and A2L refrigerants are mildly flammable. Schedule tech.
Monitor — Schedule Soon
Rattling during startup or shutdown
Loose screws on access panels, debris in condenser fan (leaves, sticks), loose ductwork connections, loose blower wheel set screw.
Inspect visually. Tighten loose panels. Remove debris. If rattling continues, schedule service — loose blower wheel can cause failure.
Monitor — Schedule Soon
Rising energy bills (+15–25% unexplained)
Dirty coils (reduces heat transfer efficiency), low refrigerant, aging compressor losing efficiency, duct leaks, thermostat miscalibration.
Replace filter, check thermostat, confirm vents open. If bill remains high, annual tune-up will diagnose the issue.
Monitor — Schedule Soon
Clicking at startup (compressor won't start)
Failed start capacitor (most common), failed contactor, low voltage, compressor hard start (thermal overload tripping), or compressor failure.
Check breaker. If tripped, reset once. If it trips again — do not reset. Capacitor replacement is a fast, inexpensive fix if caught early.
Monitor — Schedule Soon
Musty or mold smell from vents
Mold/mildew growing on evaporator coil or in drain pan, dirty ductwork, moisture in air handler from oversized system (doesn't dehumidify enough).
Replace filter. Flush drain line. In DFW humidity, coil mold is common — a coil cleaning and UV light installation can eliminate it long-term.
Section 7

Efficiency Ratings Explained

These numbers directly affect your monthly electric bill in DFW — where summer cooling can run 5–6 months per year.

SEER2
Seasonal Energy Efficiency Ratio 2
New test standard (2023+) for cooling efficiency. Replaces SEER. Measures BTU of cooling per watt-hour over a season. Minimum in South: 15 SEER2 (≈16.2 SEER old). Higher = better.
SEER
Seasonal Energy Efficiency Ratio
Legacy rating still used to describe older systems. Min. was 14 SEER in South. Good: 16–18. Excellent: 20+. Each 2 SEER points ≈ 10–12% energy savings. Very high SEER (24+) for mini-splits.
EER2
Energy Efficiency Ratio 2
Cooling efficiency at a specific condition (95°F outdoor, 80°F/50% RH indoor). More relevant for DFW peak summer than SEER2 since we have sustained extreme heat — not seasonal averages.
HSPF2
Heating Seasonal Performance Factor 2
Heat pump heating efficiency rating. BTU of heat per watt-hour over the heating season. Minimum 7.5 HSPF2. Good: 9+. DFW winters are mild — heat pumps are very cost-effective here vs. gas furnaces.
AFUE
Annual Fuel Utilization Efficiency
Gas furnace efficiency — % of fuel converted to heat. Minimum 80% in South. High-efficiency: 90–98%. In mild DFW winters, 80% AFUE is often sufficient — 96% may not justify the cost difference.
COP
Coefficient of Performance
Ratio of heating/cooling output to energy input. COP 3.0 = 300% efficiency (moves 3 units of heat per 1 unit of electricity used). Heat pumps: COP 2.5–4. Geothermal: COP 3–5. Gas furnace: max 1.0.

Section 8

Repair vs. Replace

Use the $5,000 Rule as a starting point: multiply the repair cost by the system's age. If that number exceeds $5,000, replacement is usually the better financial choice.

✓ Repair When...
  • System is under 10 years old and well-maintained
  • Repair cost is less than 50% of replacement cost
  • Minor components: capacitor, contactor, thermostat, drain line
  • System is still R-410A (refrigerant available for years)
  • System has had no major repairs in the past 3 years
  • You plan to sell the home within 2–3 years (buyer wants working system)
→ Replace When...
  • System is 15+ years old (residential) or 12+ (commercial)
  • Compressor failure on R-22 system — refrigerant too expensive
  • Repair cost × age exceeds $5,000 (the 5,000 Rule)
  • System is under 13 SEER — new system pays back quickly in DFW
  • Multiple repairs in a single season — the "death spiral" of old equipment
  • Compressor failure + evaporator coil failure simultaneously
  • Cracked heat exchanger — CO risk (always replace)
DFW Replacement Costs (2024–2025 market)
2-ton system (1,000–1,400 sq ft): $4,500–$7,500 installed. 3-ton (1,500–2,000 sq ft): $5,500–$9,500. 4-ton (2,000–2,600 sq ft): $6,500–$11,000. 5-ton (2,600–3,200 sq ft): $7,500–$13,000. Premium variable-speed systems add $2,000–$4,000. These include labor, equipment, permit, and startup.
Section 9

Dallas-Fort Worth Specific Considerations

DFW's climate is uniquely demanding on HVAC systems. What works in Atlanta or Phoenix may not be the right choice here.

DFW Climate Facts
Why DFW is harder on HVAC than almost anywhere in the US
Dallas averages 65+ days above 95°F per year. Summer nights rarely drop below 75°F, meaning your AC never fully "rests." Combine that with 60–75% relative humidity in summer, hail-prone severe storms, and expansive clay soil that shifts your equipment pads — and you have the most demanding HVAC environment in the continental US outside of Phoenix and Miami.
Heat: 100–115°F peaks
Outdoor condensers lose 15–25% capacity above 95°F. Oversizing isn't optional — it's survival. Min. 15 SEER2 required; 18+ recommended for DFW.
Hail Damage
DFW averages 5–7 significant hail events per year. Hail bends condenser fins (reducing efficiency) and can dent compressor covers. Check after every storm — file homeowner's insurance claim if severe.
Clay Soil Movement
DFW's expansive clay soil shrinks in drought (summer) and swells in rain (spring). Concrete unit pads crack and tilt — unlevel compressors wear faster. Check and relevel every 2–3 years.
Drain Line Clogs
DFW's high summer humidity means your system removes 1–3 gallons of moisture per hour. Algae grows fast in warm drain lines — monthly bleach treatment is essential, not optional.
Capacitor Failure Rate
Capacitors fail at extreme heat. In DFW, the industry average is 4–7 years of capacitor life vs. 10+ years in cooler climates. Budget for proactive capacitor replacement every 5 years.
Recommended SEER for DFW
Code minimum is 15 SEER2. For DFW's 6-month cooling season, 18–20 SEER2 systems typically pay back in 5–8 years vs. minimum efficiency. Variable-speed (20+ SEER2) also dramatically improves humidity control.
DFW Utility Rebates — Don't Leave Money on the Table
Oncor Electric Delivery offers rebates for qualifying high-efficiency HVAC equipment. Check oncor.com for current programs. Federal tax credit (25C): 30% of qualifying equipment cost, up to $600 for central AC or $2,000 for heat pumps. Ask your HVAC contractor for a Manufacturer's Certification Statement to claim the credit.
Section 10

Commercial HVAC — Key Differences

Commercial systems are fundamentally more complex — and the cost of failure is higher. Here's what building owners and managers need to know.

RTU Maintenance (Rooftop Units)
Commercial RTUs require quarterly filter changes, monthly belt inspections (belt-drive units), annual economizer calibration, coil cleaning twice yearly, and refrigerant checks. In DFW, rooftop exposure to UV and hail accelerates degradation — inspect cabinet seals and drain pans after every major storm. Typical service contract: $600–$1,200/unit/year.
Quarterly Service $600–$1,200/unit/yr contract
VAV Systems (Variable Air Volume)
VAV boxes modulate airflow to individual zones — each has a damper and often a reheat coil. Common issues: stuck dampers, failed actuators, VAV controller board failures, reheat coil leaks. Require BAS (Building Automation System) integration for proper diagnostics. Annual calibration of VAV boxes is essential for comfort and energy efficiency.
BAS Integration Required Annual Calibration
Preventive Maintenance Contracts
A commercial PM contract is not optional — it's business continuity insurance. PM contracts include priority service (no waiting behind residential calls in a DFW July heatwave), documented maintenance records (required for warranty and insurance), and typically save 10–25% on total HVAC costs vs. reactive-only service. Always get a contract that specifies response time SLAs.
ROI: 10–25% cost savings Priority in summer emergencies
Commercial Refrigerant Regulations
Commercial systems often use R-404A (walk-ins), R-407C (chillers), or R-410A (RTUs).
